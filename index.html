Background

Brief Background about the state of games and computers now, and how those modern conviences are different then what we started with The purpose of this project is to broaden public understanding of the burgeoning PC and console game market in the early 1980s through an immersion in a publication that addressed the games and by playing the games themselves. We want to shed some light on what it was like to base choices about games just from a magazine, sometimes the only source of information for gamers.
We use Softline magazine, which was mostly focused on Apple II games, but reviewed and advertised for other consoles, such as Atari and Commodore 64. Perhaps not as well known as other magazines, in addition to its myriad of game reviews, Softline featured quite a few letters to the editor regarding games and other magazine features. These letters help to understand the readers and gamers of the time.
Once subscribed to the email service, users will be able to peruse the magazines sent by the service and choose one game mentioned in each magazine. Only one game can be chosen in order to simulate a situation similar to that in the past where game picks were most-likely limited based on space, time, and money. Some players did not have easy access to a physical computer store that may have sold games. Mail-ordering of games was popular and therefore, it would take time to receive a game, and at prices of around $30 each (thatâ€™s about $75 today), it may not have been feasible to purchase multiple games at a time.
Much of the game history known is that of big corporations such as Nintendo and SEGA. Many of the games and companies to choose and read about in Softline are not known today. Some of the companies failed during the software Shakeout in the mid-1980s, and not simply for reasons of game quality. These games, whether good or bad, can help to understand design directions, style, popular games to clone, and a little of what was on the minds of the game makers and the players for whom they were making games.

Some of these games won't be great. Some may be confusing. Others might be classics. But we hope the games selected will give readers a sense of our pasts, both in how much has changed and how little has changed.

About

Experience the history or early personal computing through a free digital subscription to Softline, a gaming magazine from the early 1980s. Each issue will allow you to pick one of three Apple II games to test out for yourself, but be wary, the game you pick will be the only one you can afford that month!
